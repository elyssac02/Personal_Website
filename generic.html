Bio

My name is Elyssa Collins and I'm a Ph.D. Student in the Center for Geospaial Analytics at North Carolina State University. Before attending NCSU, I graduated from the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill with a B.S. in Geological Sciences.

At UNC Chapel Hill, I worked in various research labs with different disciplines, exposing me to the abundance of research that exists. My first research experience was in the Increasing Diversity and Enhancing Academia REU program in which I studied the shell species, Glycymeris americana, to analyze how shell morphometrics, age, and chemical variations in this species are affected by certain environmental conditions, such as seasurface temperatures. After realizing that I wanted my research to focus more on computer modeling and remote sensing/GIS, I joined the Climate Health and Air Quality (CHAQ) lab in the Environmental Sciences and Engineering Department in the School of Public Health at UNC Chapel Hill. In this lab, I collected observational and model output ground level ozone data to use the Bayesian Maximum Entropy (BME) method of spatial analysis to krige global ozone concentrations. The map generated from this analysis was delivered to the Global Burden of Disease to implement a health impact assessment. Lastly, I worked on a project in the Department's of Geography and Anthropology that analyzed MODIS NDVI and rainfall satellite data in order to identify locations of vegetation greening and browning in Burkina Faso, West Africa. Through these experiences and classes that I have taken at UNC and NCSU, I have gained proficient skills in GIS/remote sensing, as well as knowledge of various programming languages/softwares/ interfaces: R, Python, Google Earth Engine, GRASS GIS, ArcGIS, and Javascript/HTML/CSS (Web GIS: leaflet, Mapbox).

At the Center for Geospatial Analytics (CGA), my advisor is Ross Meentemeyer. I'm currently assisting on a project that incorporates future climate conditions into the Future Urban and Regional Environment Simulation (FUTURES), a model that projects spatially explicit urbanization patterns at discrete time steps in the future. My dissertation will focus on projecting future flooding under climate change using the General Circulation Models RCP 4.5 and 8.5 scenarios. The goal is inegrate the future flooding scenarios with FUTURES in order to simulate urbanization under future hydrologic conditions. Ultimately, this will allow for identification of adaptation and management strategies that will minimize flood risk.
